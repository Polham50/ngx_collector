\subsubsection*{Quantitative Breakdown}


\paragraph{Forward Guidance Detection Errors.}
Guidance false positives (C4) occurred at a rate of 0.0%: models identified forward-looking \textit{language} but not genuine performance \textit{guidance}. This is particularly prevalent in governance and AGM passages containing verbs such as ``will'' and ``intend'' in procedural contexts. Guidance false negatives (B3) accounted for 0.0% of guidance-annotated passages, typically where guidance was embedded within long operational paragraphs rather than a dedicated outlook section.

\paragraph{Naira and FX Boilerplate (A3).}
Nigerian corporate disclosures routinely contain extensive discussion of foreign exchange losses, naira devaluation, and CBN policy. Models frequently misclassified positive overall passages as negative when such language appeared, even when the dominant narrative was one of resilience and growth. This represents a form of anchoring on surface-level negative signals that is not exhibited by human annotators familiar with the Nigerian reporting context.

\paragraph{Conditional Guidance Collapse (B4).}
A distinctive failure mode unique to the Nigerian context: guidance statements frequently condition outcomes on FX stability, government policy, or oil price movements. Models tend to collapse these conditional statements into either positive or negative guidance, losing the epistemic hedge that human annotators preserve. This finding suggests that standard financial NLP benchmarks, which rarely contain conditional guidance at this frequency, may underestimate model limitations in frontier market contexts.

\paragraph{Cross-Model Disagreement (C5).}
We observe substantial cross-model disagreement, particularly on passages that are short, contain Nigerian-specific idioms, or involve conditional guidance. Agreement is highest on clearly positive passages (banking sector earnings releases with explicit profit figures) and lowest on oil and gas operational reviews where macro headwinds and operational improvements co-occur.
